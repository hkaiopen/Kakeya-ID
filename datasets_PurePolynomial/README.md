# Pure Polynomial Kakeya Datasets (3D, 4D, 5D)

This folder contains **five** numerical Kakeya‑type sets generated with a **pure polynomial Ginzburg–Landau equation** (no logarithmic term).  
These datasets complement the logarithmic version and provide direct numerical evidence for the variational framework used in the rigorous proof.

## Why a pure polynomial version?

The original numerical experiments used a logarithmic potential to prevent the field from vanishing on the prescribed line segments.  
The rigorous mathematical proof, however, employs a **geometric obstacle** that is analytically closer to a pure polynomial GL energy.  
To bridge this gap, we repeated the simulations using the **same GL dynamics but without the logarithmic term**.

The results demonstrate that the polynomial dynamics **can** produce Kakeya sets in higher dimensions (4D, 5D) with extreme compression.  
In 3D, the pure polynomial evolution **over‑compresses** the field to a single point that **cannot** cover all directions – a phenomenon that reveals the necessity of the information‑preserving barrier (logarithmic potential or geometric obstacle).

## Why does 3D fail with a single point?

In three dimensions, a **single grid point** together with its periodic images cannot intersect a unit segment in **every** continuous direction – the dense orbits generated by irrational slopes are not sufficiently “filling” on the 3‑torus.  
The pure polynomial GL dynamics, driven solely by the compression terms, pushes the system toward the global energy minimum – a single point – because **no information‑preserving barrier prevents over‑compression**.

The logarithmic potential (or the geometric obstacle in the proof) creates an infinite energy barrier as the field approaches zero, forcing the dynamics to retain a **minimal network of support points** (e.g., 13 points in the logarithmic 3D dataset) that guarantees full directional coverage.  
Thus, the 3D polynomial runs **succeed in extreme compression but fail in coverage**, perfectly illustrating the necessity of the information‑preserving mechanism.

In contrast, **four and five dimensions** are sufficiently “spacious” that even a single point (with its dense periodic orbits) can intersect unit segments in all tested directions, achieving both extreme compression and perfect coverage.

## Verification

Use the script `verify_kakeya.py` (located in the parent directory) to independently check measure and coverage.  
For the 3D single‑point files, the script will report **0% coverage** because even deterministic fine tracing cannot find an intersection – a genuine geometric limitation, not a numerical artifact.
